## MEMORIES OF THE WAR YEARS

I was raised in a terraced house on Albany Street, Rotherham, A once lovely tree lined street, and a happy place to  grow up in – despite the war – which of course affected me little because of my tender years.  

Uniformed men were everywhere but my dad along with many others was not called 
up because of his job in the Steel Works. Coal Miners were also exempt.

Talk everywhere was of “sacrifices” though in my childhood innocence I was never aware of any.

I remember wartime posters ”Waste not want not” “Careless talk costs lives” “Dig for Victory”, etc.

The Prime Minister Winston Churchill was on the Radio daily. Not always with good news.

It must have been as hard for the families of servicemen then - as it is today.

Barrage Balloons were everywhere, and the blackout curtains were gloomy.  Air Raid Wardens patrolled the streets at night to ensure there were no visible lights.  Sadly this did not always stop the bombs falling around Rotherham.  

I well remember going on a tramcar with Mum and Dad to Sheffield after one such raid and being horrified by the devastation. Buildings were reduced to rubble but bizarrely some brave hearts were still trying to run their businesses even though the windows had been blown out.

My uncle Alan would take me to the NAFI when he was on leave and I was very proud
To be seen with him in uniform.  He brought me a bracelet from India made of silver
Coins and I wore it even to bed.

Many of our childhood Summers were spent in Clifton Park – On Sundays my Grandma Garbett, Aunty Sarah, Aunty Mary. Aunty Eileen and seven cousins, would meet in front of the bandstand to watch “Sunshine Corner” – A “Holidays at Home” initiative. 

If you sang you would be given sweets by “Uncle Archie” but I was never brave enough. On fine days we would splash around in the paddling pool, and on wet days reluctantly visit the museum  which was not as child friendly as the present one.

If we had been “good” we could have an ice cream on the way home. This would take the form of a cornet with more ice chippings than actual cream but we enjoyed the treat nevertheless.		

## GRANDPARENTS

My grandma Garbett was very kind and lived at No. 25 Fitzwilliam Rd. Rotherham
She had a lovely back garden full of vegetables – My cousins and I used to help her by picking peas and beans – the summer’s seemed long and warm in those days. Wild roses and huge colourful flowers graced her substantial front garden.

She was always dressed in black (because of widowhood or practicality I’m unsure) Her hair reached below her waist but she wore it tied back severely in a bun, over which she’d  plonk on a straw hat for outings. Her house (council owned) was always full of laughter. We loved to visit.

Joe (her husband) had died young so we never knew him (but we do have his photograph).

Grandma Tilletson lived at 20 Victoria Road, Parkgate.  She also had long hair
which she allowed me to brush and play “hairdressers”.

Her living room was typically Victorian – there was an uncomfortable horsehair settee which scratched your legs when seated, a dark wooden dresser full of ornaments which would of course be today’s “antiques”. 

Her fireplace however, was quite splendid and boasted a brass fender with 
matching fire irons. A noisily ticking clock which chimed on the hour always took pride of place on the mantelpiece.  

Huge religious pictures covered the walls (some I found quite scary). I also remember a side table with a bird cage on it, but sadly there was never a bird in it. She always seemed very sad and care worn but I later realised this was due to poor health as she died of pneumonia at  aged  only sixty (which shows how far health care has improved since then).

The streets of Rotherham in those days were mostly cobbled, with more horse drawn vehicles on the road than cars.  Our milkman drove a horse and cart with churns stacked on the back.

He always made such a racket in the mornings we never needed an alarm clock.
Our soot covered coal man could also be heard a mile off, he would haul Hessian sacks from the back of his cart and tip the coal in front of each property. The man of the house would be responsible for shovelling the load down the cellar grate (usually after a shift of work). 

## HEALTH CARE

The air in Rotherham was badly polluted not only due to the coal fires but from the smoke that belched out from the many industrial steelworks and factories. 

Of course this caused many bronchial problems and the doctors surgeries were always
full of patients requesting help which usually took the form of  a prescribed standard cough linctus.

This would be made up by a pharmacist & passed through a hatch in the wall for collection on the way out. 

The surgeries were depressing places – people sat on crude benches waiting for a buzzer to ring. When a doctor was free. People aired their complaints to anyone who cared to listen – probably to pass the time.  Privacy was not the rule as in the Health Centres of today.

Club Feet were not an uncommon sight and I pitied seeing so many “hair lips” on people of my own age, these afflictions must have been very hard to bear.

## OLD NEIGHBOURS

I remember lovely caring people – A Mrs Hoare whose husband worked at a Flour Mill
he was always covered in white dust, an occupational hazard.  His wife made Easter eggs for my brother Peter and I in her tiny kitchen. We had to bring our own chocolate bar. The exciting part was being allowed to watch while she iced on our names.

We were blessed with good neighbours and most of them became life long friends.
All working men (including my dad) served in the “Home Guard” after work.  The women
would do their bit by constantly knitting socks or pegging rugs, inactivity was not an option.

A car wasn’t the “must have” item it is today. Telephones were also a luxury denied to the majority. Therefore, it was necessary - on the whole- for people to communicate “face to face “.
											         

## EARLY DAYS CHRISTMAS                    								

Our old Christmas tree was in a pathetic state, the branches were few and far between
Candle holders perched lopsidedly on the end of each tattered branch
The decorations though were magical and had obviously been treasured over the years.

I remember the excitement as each bauble was brought out year after year.
 
Mum let me “help” and be her food taster on the many baking sessions before the big day.

The kitchen smelled of spices and cinnamon which were sprinkled over apple rings before they went in the oven -  along with the usual tarts and mince pies of course.

Pillowcases were always put at the end of our beds on Christmas Eve . In the morning we would drag them now fully loaded to our parents room and share the opening of them. 

When young I was given dolls –and particularly liked a black celluloid one (Diana).
My brother (ten years younger) was given a dog in the form of a pyjama case (Woof).  
He hated to be parted from him and took him to bed every night.  He later progressed to noisier “boy toys” which  didn’t go down well  with me.

When a little older my favourite present of all time was a mahogany desk that dad had made for me by a friend from work. It was huge with a sloping lid but I could barely reach the footrest.  It housed a growing book collection and was my pride and joy.

We always went to Mass on Christmas day and joined in with the carol singing (crows among nightingales).  On the way home we would call into Aunty Mary’s for coffee and exchange presents. My cousin Alan was the baby of the family and was made much of.  I would later become his chief baby sitter.

Christmas meals were special but I remember them as much for the snowy white table cloths and “best crockery” used for these occasion, as for the food we enjoyed from the loaded table.

## JUNIOR SCHOOL

One of my earliest childhood memories at the age of five was attending Wellgate
School.  This was an old Victorian building. Inside the classroom walls were painted a depressingly dark green colour.
Our sloping desks were scarred with the initials of ex pupils deeply carved into them.

There was an inkwell on top of each.  We were provided with pens, nibs & much needed blotting paper. When I look back it seemed more Dickension than Victorian, the books were well thumbed.

Children should be Seen and not heard! “Fingers on lips” was the order of the day and cane’s were always visible and used should anyone dare to break the rules.

Lessons were often interrupted in wartime Britain. We would sit in the air raid shelters in our gas masks whenever the sirens went off and sing “Ten Green Bottles” very loudly to cover the sound of falling bombs, to us it was all good fun and we welcomed the respite from lessons.

One day Miss Beevis (she of the lank hair and wart covered nose) said she was tired of sharpening pencils and would smack the next child who broke one.  Of course it had to be me!

To avoid her anger, when her back was turned I ran out of the classroom and kept on running. I was a timid child which illustrates how frightened I was. 
After explaining what had happened at home my mum, after words with “Miss” made sure I never went back there again.

I was not sorry to see the place completely demolished in the seventies, along with it’s archaic rules. 

Apart from this unhappy start to my school life, I then attended St. Bedes School.

The atmosphere there was so different. All classrooms were glass partitioned and lined with colourful posters. At play time we enjoyed milk from half pint bottles with cardboard tops.

I made many friends there (some I still see today).  Because it was a catholic school we all attended church on Sundays so our parents also bonded. There I looked forward to attending the classes and enjoyed being a brownie every Friday evening. (our only after school activity).

## POST WAR CELEBRATIONS

The streets were festooned with flags.  Posters reading “Welcome Home Son” were displayed in many windows, and noisy street parties celebrated the occasion. A happy time for those who came back – in spite of the ill fitting “demob” suits.  Sadly there were heavy losses as evidenced by the Cenotaph in Clifton Park.

I well remember walking into Rotherham Town Centre on VE Day with my parents.

An illuminated bus was driven in front of the Parish Church with local dignitaries on board. I was hoisted onto dad’s shoulders for a better view.  Patriotic songs were sung with feeling.   

## SENIOR SCHOOL

I  enjoyed senior school - even though this took place in a very old damp ridden building with fungi growing  out of the classroom walls. Nevertheless it was a very happy place. Unfortunately I never shone at anything but English, my other results were mediocre to say the least.

However, In my final year I surprisingly made “head girl”.

I longed to be in the school choir but was one of the first to be tapped on the shoulder (the obvious  signal to drop out). I attempted to join in the next day hoping not to be noticed - I WAS.

My “work experience” was spent in the adjoining children’s nursery. The 3 year olds wore little Blue overalls tied at the back with white ribbon which I fastened for them on arrival.

Toys would then be put out for the day – there was a sand pit, a water trough and lots of little wooden toys. A beautiful rocking horse stood in the corridor but sadly no-one was allowed to play on it – such a shame I always thought. 

The tots would be put down for an afternoon nap on little wooden cots and I would tuck them in with a blanket (one or two needed persuasion). I particularly remember identical twins being holy terrors – Tim and Martin!

Miss Crofton, the teacher in charge taught me “children need to feel safe with a little discipline and a lot of love”.  She was right!

## ALBANY STREET

A special treat on Saturday morning’s was to be taken to Davy’s Café in town.
The smell of Coffee permeated the building.  White cloths were on every table and the silverware gleamed. Waitresses in spotless white caps and aprons took orders. We had to be on our best behaviour. From there we would usually go on to the old Rotherham library, There were steps leading up to  the Building and a revolving door that led you into a parquet floored room.

Heavily bound books were stacked on wooden oak shelves from floor to ceiling.

Complete silence was expected, I would sit at a small table while my mother chose her books. Eventually I enrolled at the Children’s library next door and was thrilled to have my own ticket. There I discovered the likes of Noel Stretfield, Nancy Drew, and Angela Brazil.

At the age of 14 I was chosen to select the books the girls would read in school leisure times. A great privilege.

In those days there were five cinema houses in Town, the most popular one being the Odean. Here two films would be shown an A Movie (in colour if you were lucky), And a B Movie.

After the obligatory cartoon and trailers for the following week, an organist would 
perform while you had your refreshments. Going to the pictures was a lengthy process and took up  most of your day. 

The Whitehall Cinema had a café attached and for that reason we went there as a family some Saturdays, depending on what shift dad was on.

Ice-Creams were sold from the front of the screen by a lady with a tray. We queued patiently for our tubs which came with a little wooden spoon. 

The remaining picture houses were:-  The Cinema, The Empire and The Premier.
The ones that haven’t been demolished are now either Bingo Halls or nefarious nightclubs.    

## SHOPPING

Many shops prospered after the war because the men were now mostly fully employed by The British Steel Company and others. Money was more readily available and people had the “feel good factor” once more.

Furniture would often have “Utility  Marks” on them – but this was acceptable due to 
the shortage of materials post war.

There was a huge Co-Op where we did most of our shopping with the obligatory ration books.

Butter was served by an assistant who sliced your portion with a spatula before wrapping it in greaseproof paper. Sugar was always packaged in blue bags.  

Biscuits were displayed in huge tins with glass fronts – although choice was limited.  These would have to be weighed by hand.  The whole shopping experience was labour intensive – the longest queue was always at the bacon slicing machine.  

Eventually, you would hand over cash and the assistant, would transport it on a sort of pulley system to a clerk who sat in an elevated position at the far end of the store, she would return your receipt and change in the same manner.

My mum used her Co-Op Dividend to buy our shoes and clothes, but rarely anything for herself.

Other shops I remember on Wellgate were the Iron Mongers, Cobblers and Bakeries, all 
Independently owned.  There were, Butchers, Fish Merchants, Cigarette and Sweet Shops. The pride in these individual establishments was obvious. Customers were treated as friends.

I remember the local Smithy who wore a leather apron. I watched wide eyed as horseshoes were being changed  - I was convinced the poor animals were being hurt as the nails were driven through their hooves, and no one could persuade me otherwise.

Rationing was still necessary but a friend shared her cream biscuits & other goodies with me “black market” my mum would sniff disapprovingly. I was disappointed & couldn’t understand why  she wasn’t happy for me.  But “SPIVS” took advantage of the wartime shortages and grew rich on shady dealings, "Decent people" mum explained should only have what they were entitled to.            

## ACTIVITIES 

 Youth Clubs were commonplace and very popular with parents who probably knew we would be 
kept out of mischief for at least a couple of hours.
These were mainly held in church halls where you played table tennis and learned to dance to 
gramophone records. We paid a small entrance fee – presumably for the tea and biscuits provided.
Outings to Derbyshire in battered old coaches were organised by the Youth Leaders.
 I loved these. We would see many places of interest, but  particularly enjoyed visiting the likes of
Chatsworth House, and the famous Blue John Mines.

## EMPLOYMENT

Leaving  school at 15 helped you grow up very quickly. After interview I was offered the position of
Junior Clerk with The Plessey Company, who made aeroplane parts for the likes of Metro Vickers and
Hawker Hunter.  My starting salary was 35 shilling per week.  I was expected to start work 
at 7.30 am in the mornings and finish at 5.30 pm. (Long hours compared to school days for 
one so young, although I thought nothing of it at the time). 
 I was not made to feel particularly welcome initially because the woman who was “training” me
felt she had been imposed upon and I was merely an extra duty. She constantly reminded me of this.

The journey to and from work was a long one.  After walking a mile into Rotherham, I then had to 
catch a trackless bus. The poles on this type of transport sometimes became detached from the 
overhead cables. The hapless driver would then have to get out and re-connect them – The 
passengers were rarely sympathetic even in the coldest of winters. They were all in a hurry.    
After work I walked or ran all the way home.  My mum was lovely and would have a meal ready for 
me.  It was good to be back at the end of the day and the house was always warm and cosy thanks 
to the coal fires.
After tea we would listen to the Radio together and she would ask about my day.
								
Getting used to long hours was hard and I was always in bed early.  I remember crying into my
pillow a great deal knowing I would have to face my dreaded tormentor the following day.
She seemed to delight in undermining the little confidence I had.

In those days you would be interviewed by the Labour Exchange to monitor your progress
after the first month in work.  Here I told the lady interviewing me that the women on 
the factory floor (where the office was situated) used the most awful  language.   To her credit she 
didn’t laugh  but instead  rang the Personnel Officer of the  firm on my  behalf. 

In my naivety I thought she would ask the offender to curb her language.

In fact, a week later I was transferred into a lovely modern office - a typing pool - where I
immediately felt at home with the other girls.  Another bonus was that I could start work at 8.30 – 
and finish at 5.30.  Progress indeed. 
     
One day the Personnel Officer (Miss Jones) asked if I would like to have Shorthand Lessons.
These were to be held in the Board Room during working hours. Fourteen of us began the course
but after 3 months the numbers had dwindled so the classes were cancelled.

However, the firm arranged for myself and two other girls to have private lessons two evenings
 per week. With encouragement from Mum I also enrolled at Night School on a three year course 
which included shorthand and typing.

We bashed the old Underwood typewriters to the tune of William Tell (To learn speed & 
Rhythm). The racket with thirty girls to a class was deafening, but I was in my element because 
this was what I wanted to do, and I eventually passed the course with flying colours (which I’d
like to think compensated for my lack of achievements at school) .I was certainly a late starter.			
By this time I was ambitious and wanted to get out of the typing pool.

This took me 18 months when I then became Secretary to the Production Manager.  I enjoyed this
position for seven happy years until the firm re-located to Swindon – although along with others
I was offered employment down south, I was never tempted & very few of the staff actually moved.	

## COURTSHIP

It was at a Plessey’s dance held in the Assembly Rooms where Paul and I met.

He asked me to dance (I’ve had to do the asking ever since) – I was conned!

We shared a coffee (pre wine bar days) and after spending some time together agreed to meet at 
the cinema next evening. 

We started dating regularly, sometimes with other couples, there always seemed to be a party of 
some description to frequent. It was during this carefree time that we became engaged –we chose 
a double diamond ring together and life was wonderful.

(There was a beer named Double Diamond at the time) One day I left my ring lying around at work
The boss thought I deserved a lesson for my carelessness and hid it from me.  After a
frantic search, he came clean & the whole office sang the jingle “A Double Diamond Works
Wonders”.  

Lesson learned!

Conscription was a must at that time, which was untimely for Paul because he had just been
offered promotion at work.  However, he signed on for three years with The Royal Dragoon Guards
 when only 18 months was necessary, I was really hurt by this, at seventeen 3 years seemed  a 
lifetime.  

This was at the time of the Suez Crisis, and quite worrying for service men and their families.
 We exchanged letters while he was serving in Germany, but I was lonely and unhappy so I wanted 
To go dancing with  friends.– Then fiancés were expected to sit at home and wait - So
 trying to do what I thought right, I broke off the engagement. How young & thoughtless I was.

Because my grandma lived across the road from Plessey’s I used to go there for lunch
and get thoroughly spoiled.  One day Paul’s mum (who also worked at Plessey’s in those days) came 
to tell me that Paul had suffered a serious skiing accident and she thought I should know.  Of course I 
 was really upset and wrote and told him so.

On his next leave  he came to visit me but as usual I was out dancing at  the Clifton Hall with
 friends from work.  Marina was with me then and is a good friend to this day.
             		
Following a ”heart to heart” with my mum who always cared for him, she simply said
“go and find her Paul” – He did and the rest is history.  In truth I believe the separation		
was a good thing because we both did a lot of growing up in that time and it made us
realise we were soul mates.

When Paul came out of the Army we decided to marry and started saving in earnest.

I was working at a finance company earning £8 per week and Paul was earning £11
as a bricklayer. Because of his skiing accident in the forces, he was granted an army pension and this 
made it financially possible for us to marry in spite of our low salaries.

At that time (1959) Brinsworth Garden Estate was being built, along with the Three Magpies Hotel.

We took a bus ride to view the plans and fell in love with the bungalows.  We put down
a deposit and the builders promised to have No.36 finished by our Wedding Day
arranged for the 5th December.

## OUR WEDDING 1959

We married on a surprisingly sunny day for December at St. Bedes, Masborough.

It was a modest affair and could hardly be described as Wedding of the Year as we had long 
decided any savings would go on our longed for home.

My  wedding dress was made by Marina’s sister who charged me very little for making
up the white flocked nylon material.   My bridesmaids apart from Marina were cousins.
Our ‘taxis’ were supplied by friends and cousins. The only record we have of our big day
Are snapshots taken by Uncle Norman.  (ahh!) Stuart (Paul’s brother in law) was our best man.
Thanks to my mum’s lovely neighbours and Paul’s mum, Tea was arranged for 100 people 
Our reception took place at the Eastwood Hotel – a popular venue in those days, but now long gone.
My wedding cake and flowers were lovely thanks to friends of mum’s (Mrs.Guillam and Mrs.Guest).
We had a dance afterwards and everything went according to our simple plans.

As we had no transport we were driven to a very cold and damp Derwent Crescent by 
Paul’s aunty Elsie– she brought her children along and we spent the evening playing cards.  A 
Different Wedding night to say the least! – but there no spare cash for a honeymoon.
Against everyone’s advice we moved into the bungalow too soon. Instead of the lovely nightdress
I had, the night was spent in Paul’s army issue pyjamas. Paul suffered a bad back laying 
lino in the un-dried out property and we both suffered heavy colds.  Served us right!

On our first day of married life we had visitors – Paul’s aunty Elizabeth & Uncle Frank
had brought us a Lloyd Loom bedroom chair.  We had no tea to offer them and the
shops were closed so they had cocoa from a goody bag – provided by mum of course.
I had given no thought to provisions in all the excitement – A domesticated Goddess I never was
and  had clearly set out as I meant to carry on.

We furnished the bungalow modestly but to us it was like being in heaven with the
door shut.  Both parents helped with gifts of furniture, carpets etc.  Our relations
were also generous and chipped in with bedding and curtains, we were lucky to
have such a good start and really appreciated the help given.

The cost of Our first home was £19,995 – a fortune to us, Aunty Tet gave us the loan of £20 to
secure the sale.  We repaid it of course and took her chocolates  (we now realise) as an inadequate 
thank you. (She celebrates her ninetieth birthday this year).

We took out a mortgage out with Bradford and Bingley and our rent was £9.10 shillings and 8 pence.
We now realise how green we were not to round the amount up to £10 but paid the exact sum.

## NINETEEN SIXTIES

Pipe Smoking - Prime Minister Harold Wilson - was in power in the sixties but all I remember of him 
was seeing regular photographs in the press of him sporting a Gannex Raincoat.  Rumour had it that 
the said company subscribed heavily to the Labour Party in those days.

This was supposedly the time of The Hippies and Free Love – It bypassed us completely- honestly!

The Beatles became the rage along with The Rolling Stones and the dark eye-lashed singer Dusty 
Springfield – who certainly boosted the sale of black mascara for the cosmetic industry.

Mary Quant Minis were the height of fashion – I remember wearing them with white boots
which Mum thought to be “a bit daring”. Paul was into drape suits and blue suede shoes. Yes really!
At this time feminist Germaine Greer was urging women to burn their bra’s (I could never
see the sense in that and was determined to hang on to mine).

## 36 DERWENT CRESC.

As newlyweds we enjoyed life to the full.

In those days we had no television but had saved up for a small radio and Hi-Fi Unit.
Our winter nights were spent huddled round the fire listening in. It would be some time 
Before we could afford a television or central heating, but we had free coal when others weren’t so 
Lucky .We shared our loads with neighbours.

I must confess - I had no love of the “white thing” in the kitchen but nevertheless learned to
cook in a fashion with Paul as my guinea pig - Lots of pans were burned in the process!

Our Saturday visit to the Co-Op would buy us a Managers Special – A chicken, a bag of potatoes
along with seasonal vegetables – this cost us seven shillings and sixpence – a bargain!      

Our neighbours (Audrey & Brian) were also newly married so we had much in common.

We also got to know (through attending church) Patrick & Win, at NO42.  Win was a teacher
and had volunteered scripture lessons for the children. We quickly made friends with both couples.

Across the road from us Mr. & Mrs. Gardiner “adopted” us. Their daughter had emigrated to Aus.
 and they missed her terribly. Mr. Gardiner was Headmaster at nearby Catcliffe School.
 We were also supported by another couple Mr. & Mrs. Mead who helped us with gardening advice  
 sometimes lending us their lawn mower and other tools before we could afford our own.

On Saturday nights we would regularly meet up with friends –(Marina & Doug, and Sheila & Ray)
We mostly went dancing,   or I should say attended dances as Paul was as much into dancing as
 I was to cooking.  We sometimes enjoyed meals out-Chinese mostly for some reason.
we rarely went out mid week due to lack of cash so we looked forward to these outings. 

## 1962 

By now we were both keen to start a family – I was working at BSC at the time, but
before I left work we decided to have a very belated honeymoon in Scarborough.

we stayed in a very modest bed sit – The landlady put us on the top floor thinking
we really were on honeymoon – In fact I was pregnant by then and could have done
without the stairs.  We were both glad to leave early, and left Scarborough mid week.

On the 25th September, Paul’s Mum and Dad came to see how I was progressing
with my labour.

The lounge carpet needed shampooing and Paul’s Mum helped me with this
 until tea time. We enjoyed our meal – until the sweet – then it was a dash to hospital in the car

Richard was born AS EXPECTED at midnight. Punctuality has not been his strong point since!

We were very proud parents and decided we wanted another child.  David was born at
Home in Derwent Crescent on the 19th April – This was a perfect day for me 
our family was now complete.   

He should have been Andrew Peter but Paul got it wrong!

However, I’m pleased he did – David means beloved – enough said!

Paul worked hard and was a good provider, but when Richard & David were small
 Mum took care of them a few hours daily while I typed for a secretarial  bureau from home.

## MY RETURN TO WORK		

With the boys well settled in school  I applied for jobs at BOC in Brinsworth and Edgar Allen in 
Sheffield.  I was offered  both but chose to go to Edgar Allen.  I was nervous because I was to work            
for the Managing Director of a new company to be formed within the group

On my first day at the office, my new boss Mr. Sherwood met me in a room that was bare except for 
a typewriter In the middle of the floor.  He apologised and explained there had been no-one to 
organise things and if anyone needed a secretary he did.

But not to worry “Bert” would take me to an office supplier where I could buy what was needed
He then dashed off to a Meeting leaving me completely bewildered.

Bert turned out to be the company chauffer.  He turned up looking very smart in full uniform
peaked cap and gaiters – I was impressed!  His instructions he said were to take me shopping. 
 I was given no budget so made the most of it.  When the invoice came I felt quite embarrassed.
 
The Secretaries and Senior Managers of the group were really valued and had a lovely dining room 
separated from the main canteen.  The meals were lovely and the waitresses “silver service trained”.
There was a full drinks cabinet which we appreciated but this was never abused as we worked too 
hard and needed clear heads.  Christmas however, was an exception to our self imposed rules.

I found the position challenging to say the least and was soon to learn that It wasn’t all shorthand & 
typing.   When visitors came to the premises I had to organise hotel and travel requirements, this 
was expected of course, but  I was once asked to took home the firm’s  Union jack to wash it before 
a party of Japanese visitors arrived. For good measure I also washed the first aid room screened 
curtains. Multi tasking it would be called today. 

As the company prospered 3 other directors were promoted from within the group
and I eventually took  on more responsibility.  I used temporary help when needed.     
There were perks however, Paul and I enjoyed many lavish company events   
mostly held in the Cutler’s Hall in Sheffield. We were often wined and dined in style.
However when the Group was taken over by the Aurora Group, these excesses were
frowned upon and drastic changes were made.

The staff dining room disappeared (as did Bert) and social events were few and far between
These new captains of industry meant business and the workforce was greatly reduced.

Computers were installed and we Secretaries attended Sheffield College on several courses
I found these changes stressful but have since been glad of the additional training
which now comes in useful for voluntary work and personal use.

## SILVERWOOD  COLLIERY DISASTER 1966

On Thursday 3rd February, the day shift men were being transported to their place of work on
A “paddy mail” train. It was followed by a second train carrying materials which crashed into the
rear of the train carrying the men. Nine men were killed at the scene, One man died 3 days
later in hospital (Paul’s dad) and another thirty were injured.  This was a dreadful time for the
community. Suffering the loss of so many good  men was hard to bear.  

## FOSTON ON THE WOLDS

Paul’s mum had good friends to support her - who after a while- persuaded her to buy an
old pub at Foston on the Wolds called The Plough.   Fate seemed to play a part as
when the hand written deeds were examined it was found that the pub had
originally been owned by John Nettleton.   

Some coincidence as Paul’s dad was John Nettleship.                                                                

It was a lovely old white washed building standing in the middle of a pretty village.

To her credit Paul’s mum brought the old place to life – Paul and our brother in Law Ted
helped with the refurbishment. Never to do things by halves she kept chickens and sheep
On the substantial land she now owned.  She used to cater for the local farmers (who would
occasionally pay for their drinks in exchange for home grown vegetables). The village
literally “went to bed “at 9.30 - so she never had late nights to contend with (unless
we visited of course). She also catered for the local Hunt (Red jackets and all).   
Pie & Peas were her Speciality – her cooking was always popular.

Richard and David loved to go there because they could fish in nearby trout filled streams.
After a couple of years “Dot” as she was known” grew home sick and put the place up for sale
We were offered the chance to buy– but were always “town mice” at heart.  Also the boys were in
the middle of exams. (Anyhow, how would they have met Jenny & Julie and lived happily ever
 after (ish) had we made the move? - I rest my case.

The place was eventually taken over by The Caravan Club and is now probably worth a small
fortune. Grandchildren Note:  Perhaps you should visit the place one day. It was quite 
up-market last time we had sight of it and it isn’t far from Driffield.

## NETTLESHIP CONSTRUCTIION

Paul held the position of Refractories Engineer for the Hadfields Group for many years which also
Involved being responsible for maintenance and repairs at four sister companies.
When the firm closed down making everyone redundant he started working for himself under the 
above name.

His first undertaking was to build the Gables in Bramley, we temporarily moved in with dad and also
used a site caravan. The build took 12 months – he worked through a very cold winter, but
the boys were a great help labouring and I did my bit by ordering materials and organising services.
Looking back it was a great collective achievement.

It was a very happy house – great for parties and we had quite a few!  Paul made a success
of his business, the phone never stopped ringing, we needed an answer phone because we
 both worked all day.  Jenny became our trusted accountant then and was invaluable to us.

Paul went through his “home brew” phase here – until one of his concoctions
exploded in the boiler room spilling all over white sheets and turning them beetroot red.
Phew!  Paul’s “pub” came to a very abrupt end following that incident.

At this time Mrs. Fallon came into our lives to help with the cleaning (my job was very
demanding at that time having a board of directors to contend with) she was much appreciated.
Quickly she became “one of the family” and we all loved her.  She tackled the boy’s bedrooms
cheerfully and sorted out things they had stuffed under the beds – their usual way of tidying up!
Although long retired - We still exchange cards with her at Christmas.

Our good neighbours in Bramley  were Mr & Mrs. Rix who supported us in many ways when
“Nettleship’s Castle” as they liked to call it, was under construction. Her apple pies were great!
Another neighbour owned a lovely black poodle called Buzz who often took refuge with us to 
escape his owner’s annoying children who were too much at his good good age.  We still keep his photograph.  

## WOODSIDE RAVENFIELD

When our sons moved on with their own lives, the Bramley house became far too big for us
and Paul bought a piece of land at Ravenfield, once an orchard.  Here with the help of
various tradesmen he constructed a bungalow.  During the build he suffered a back injury
for a couple of weeks and I needed to hand the bricks for him to lay one at a time – we 
weren’t easily deterred.

We rented a semi detached house in Brinsworth for the duration of the build (at this time
David was taking a Management Course while working at G.R. Stein’s).  We spent a lovely
Christmas there in spite of it being a rather cramped one.
At this time Julie and Richard were living in their first home in Maltby.

Woodside was as the name suggests close to a bluebell wood and a lovely place for 
walking  but we rarely found the time.  

Julie and Richard lived here with us here for a while after they had sold their first home in Maltby
and were planning on a new build house at Aston. 

## NEW YEAR 1990

This was to have been such a special New Year for us we had “hard to come by” tickets for a
Dance at the Pheonix Rooms in Brinsworth.  It seems hard to believe now but we had a lovely
time celebrating the New Year before getting home quite late.

We must have been in bed about an hour when Paul woke me complaining of “ indigestion”
which, in fact, frighteningly turned out to be a heart attack.   Richard called an ambulance
and his dad was quickly hospitalised.  This was the longest night of my life as the doctors told me
the next 24 hours was crucial. I said a lot of prayers which thank god were answered.
After a short but worrying time in hospital, Paul was eventually allowed home.

Following the initial shock and getting back to some sort of normality, we were invited by
The nursing staff who had cared for him to join ROTHERHEART a support group for such as us.
We both gained confidence from the group and went on to make many new friends.

Paul helped new members with recovery advice and I published the monthly newsletter.
We enjoyed many outings with this Group to Theatres and National Trust Houses
Speakers would be invited to give talks each week (not always on health care – but
photography, and other hobbies of interest).  Word spread  throughout the town of the good work 
the nurses were doing and our numbers rapidly Increased.

One day after not feeling very well – Paul visited the newly opened surgery in Ravenfield.
After consultation with a doctor he was referred to a specialist in Sheffield.  It was a shock
To learn he urgently needed a triple heart by pass.  It was a very worrying time for us all.

We had been planning to move and build again, but were advised against it by the doctors 
which was how we came to buy Oak Cottage then owned by Richard.  He had to give back
word to a couple who were to buy it but there was little choice.  Once again fate played a
part in our lives. 

## HOSPITALISATION

The doctor who was in charge of Paul’s operation had actually been at school with Richard.
He was wonderful and arranged for me to stay overnight at the hospital on the night of the 
Operation. It was reassuring to know he was getting the best possible care.  His surgeon was a 
renowned Italian professor and we learned the best there was.

Recovery was a long slow process but again Paul bounced back. A stroke some years later was
Much harder for us to cope with but medical staff at Rotherham District General were there
To help us get through the worst of it, and eventually discharged him (under protest).

## RETIREMENT

The years seemed to fly by until retirement for both of us.
I took up voluntary work at first  at St. Bedes and The Writers Education Assn., then at Out of the 
Blue (A police run operation) and finally at Rotherham Aid for people in need.  I re-retired when this 
closed down due to lack of funding, many charities suffered a similar fate in 2012.
 
Paul was in his element working at Rother Valley Country Park manufacturing pewter goods.
This bothered us initially but the doctor assured us it would be a healthy occupation for him.
Together we worked on many craft fairs (organised by Richard). It seems we have always
“enjoyed” working, or rather meeting people and feeling useful.

## HOLIDAYS

We have enjoyed some memorable holidays abroad in The South of France, once with with Jenny & 
David before they married made memorable by their visiting Monte Carlo and my breaking a
Leg.  

It was also a great few years for us when we shared a holiday cottage in Cornwall with Julie
Richard while the family were growing up.  A lot of our time was spent in Looe.
 
Paul and I enjoyed many years caravanning – We made it “home from home” and
this became our bolt hole when the pressures of work got to us -which was quite often.
We used to dash from work on Friday night’s and set up the van for the weekend.
Filey was our port of call, and from there we would visit Scarborough or Whitby.
Happy days!

We changed vans over the years and could share the last one with the family
occasionally, but this was short lived as Paul’s health deteriorated.

Over the years we have enjoyed many holidays in France, Spain, Tenerife and Germany
But more recently have taken to “coach holidays” – Our favourites of these were Ireland
and Scotland. We did drive to Wales once but never felt the urge to go back – well it
does rain a lot!

Richard played the guitar from school with, it must be admitted, very little encouragement
from us. Luckily Julie was more supportive and a gifted singer to boot. 

We are now lucky enough to enjoy listening to live music quite often and have made many friends
through this since retirement.  Age it seems is no barrier here!

We have to approach the bungalow quietly sometimes after a night out because our 
neighbours are usually in bed when we get home.  Are we growing old disgracefully?

We make no apologies for being very proud of our sons, “adopted” daughters and grandchildren 
because they have each  individually given us every reason to be, and we love them for it.

It’s great when the families get together but this is not always easy now as the grandchildren – quite 
rightly – have their own agendas. 

Faith has always been important in my life and it pleases me that Paul now comes to
Church with me on Sundays. Although before he attended he always made sure I got there anyhow (a promise he faithfully kept to my mum).

We have seen many changes over the years –  A few among these are:
* The first Moon Landing. (we stayed up all night to watch this on TV).
* The first time we saw coloured television
* The first mobile phone (the size of a house brick)
* Computerisation 
* Plastic bank cards
* Decimalisation
* The introduction of OFSTEAD
* Car safety belts
* Smoking banned in public places
* Body part transplants 

These notes of my humble life will *MAYBE* be of interest to my favourite people someday.

We want St Bedes to be our final resting place - *BUT NO RUSH!*
